Lecture Snippet • Harvard University

Justice: What's the Right Thing to Do?

Episode 01: The Moral Side of Murder. A profound exploration of ethics, utilitarianism, and the value of human life through the lens of Professor Michael Sandel.

The Thought Experiments

Sandel uses these famous hypotheticals to test our moral intuitions.

1. The Trolley Problem

A runaway trolley is hurtling toward five workers. You can turn it onto a side track where only one worker is present. Do you kill one to save five?

2. The Fat Man

This time, you're on a bridge. To stop the trolley from killing five workers below, you must push a fat man off the bridge onto the tracks. Is it still "better that one die so five live"?

3. The Transplant Surgeon

Five patients need organ transplants to live. A healthy man comes in for a checkup. Do you "yank out his five organs" to save the five? Why does this feel different?

The Two Paths of Morality

The lecture identifies two core modes of moral reasoning that emerged from the student debates.

Consequentialist

Morality is located in the results of an act. The right thing to do depends on the state of the world that follows. "Better that five should live, even if one must die."

Philosophy: Utilitarianism (Jeremy Bentham)

Categorical

Morality is located in certain absolute requirements, duties, and rights, regardless of the consequences. Some acts are just "categorically wrong," even if they save lives.

Philosophy: Deontology (Immanuel Kant)

The Queen vs. Dudley & Stephens

"A sadder story of disaster at sea was never told..."

In 1884, four men were stranded in a lifeboat in the South Atlantic for 20 days. Starving and desperate, Captain Dudley and First Mate Stephens killed the youngest crew member, the cabin boy Richard Parker, to survive. They ate his remains and were eventually rescued.

The Question: Was it morally permissible to sacrifice the one to save the three, given the extreme necessity? Or is murder always murder, regardless of the stakes?

"Philosophy estranges us from the familiar, not by supplying new information, but by provoking a new way of seeing." — Michael Sandel
